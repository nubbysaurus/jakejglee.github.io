background

Connecting Li-ion battery cells (18650) in series is a good way to power electronics for applications ranging from LEDs to electric vehicles. To build a custom battery pack, you only need to connect however many battery cells in series required to meet your application's voltage spec, connect cells in parallel to meet your applicatoins's power budget/max current spec, get an appropriate charger and regulator, and WHAM: you've got a battery pack that can power anything you could possibly imagine.

If it were that easy, this wouldn't be as much fun.

The above fairy tale makes some big assumptions:

  1. Each battery is identical.

    2. While battery makers will give you a good idea for a battery cell's typical voltage range and capacity, no two cells are perfectly the same. This necessitates smarter ways of judging a cell's state of charge (SOC) than waiting for it to reach a certain voltage.

  2. Charge distributes evenly to each cell.

    3. Aside from each cell's uniqueness, factors like temperature, age, and SOC change the mobility of charge carriers within a cell. Without taking these into account, it is hard to gauge battery capacity. If you connect a charger across a series set of cells alone, you will end up with some cells with too much charge and others with too little charge, a recipe for battery failure and even explosions.
