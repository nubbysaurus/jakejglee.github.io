background

It is hard to make anything without access to a nice workspace. As my housemate and I reclaimed our cluttered garage for the valuable space, we disassembled large wooden structures that left us with a ton of scrap wood. I called dibs and thus this journey into woodworking began.

break it down

mk.i

Rewind about a year: I put together my first attempt at a workbench right after I moved in. This rough draft was hastily assembled, crude, and could best be described as abstract art. It was too wobbly, narrow, cluttered, and uneven to be very useful, so I was happy to tear it down when we cleaned out the garage.

mk.ii

My next attempt at a workbench that could even be described as woodworking started. I found a design online by Ana White that I liked, made a couple modifications to meet my space and functional needs, and started cutting parts with my housemate's miter saw. The design for this version was a big improvement from my previous mess, but it didn't explain woodworking in absolute beginner-friendly language (like how to form perfect 90° joints in wood, for instance). After assembling its frame, I was concerned that it was still too uneven and unsturdy and decided to start over. On top of not knowing basic woodworking principles, I hadn't considered how oblong my 2x4s were, in part because I hadn't stored them properly during the freak lightning storms that happened here in the middle of summer.

Unable to achieve right-angled joints, I tried to dismantle my first frame and start over. However, since I stripped some screws trying to take it apart and had wood-glued its nearly-perpendicular joints together, it is now helping to cradle my tomato plants in my garden, shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1. mkii keeps good company amongst tomatoes.

mk.iii

Researching online, I learned how to use my miter saw (it rotates?!) to make 45° cuts and create stronger, more elegant joints for my frames. During my research, I stumbled on a video of someone using cable ties rather than big clamps to keep their table's frame together as the wood glue at its joints dried. Since I don't (*yet) have clamps, I followed suit and came back about a day later and was happy with my frames this time.

Figure 2. Mitered frames look real nice for mkiii.

Next, I did some trial and error, as well as some freestyle wood balancing/leaning methods, to attach the legs to my frames. Convinced that tables were level and equal in height, I reinforced them and placed them in their staging area. Here, I attached them to each other to form the miter saw's stand and rechecked that everything was level. Unfortunately, the miter saw was too high up on its stand since I'd mistakenly raised the frames of each table ~1/4" too high. My neighbor Shawn suggested that I buy some 1/4" plywood for the saw's frame and reinforce it with studs where the saw attaches. Thankful for the wisdom, I grabbed some from the store and got to cutting.

Figure 3. mkiii's skeleton (left); mkiii with studs and some surfaces (right).

Here, I realized why my parents probably didn't want me to take woodshop. Borrowing my dad's circular saw, I sketchily cut through 1/2" plywood mounted on top of evenly spaced 2x4s laid on their sides. Somehow leaving that process with all of my fingers, I was energized and ready to finish.

I attached the plywood surfaces to its companion frames, completed the shelf for my electronics section of the bench, hung a couple of power strips, attached some lights, and called it a day...and then remembered to clean up.

Figure 4. mkiii shining in the darkness of a pristine garage.

Since completing my workspace, I've added a roll-out toolbox and a wet/dry vaccuum under the miter saw to catch sawdust. Overall, this bench is working very well for my needs.
