bellsprout

modified : 2023-06-19

background

At the end of every January for the past three years, I have wanted to step up my seed germination game. Since my time volunteering at Spade and Plow, I've been wondering how to cheaply get the best yields from a scalable seed germination setup. Two years ago, I bought a cheap-o germination pad online (like this one and got some seeds to germinate unevenly in my tray. I found it hard to keep the tray appropriately damp as well, and I managed to both dry the tray out when I forgot to water it and attracted cockroaches when I flooded it. Additionally, the cheap-o heat pad that I purchased did not have the ability to change heat output. More control over heat output would allow me to germinate seeds that require different temperatures/humidities to sprout, plus I could react to changes in ambient temperature.

design

Whilst digging through a/[my] pile of E-waste, I very conveniently came across a board that was previously used to power heat tape on a probably-long-gone test fixture (below). This board comes complete with ICs for monitoring temperature via four optional thermo- couples, which largely takes care of my temperature regulation needs. I even dug up a complementary Arduino Micro uC to test the entire board. I am currently recreating the board's schematic in KiCAD, and my analysis so far is that it was well thought-out.

This board is powered by two power supplies: the heating circuitry runs at 5V max, while the digital bits are at 3.3V, which is generated by the Arduino through a USB-micro connector. I am a little concerned about connecting to the Arduino through my computer while running the heat board in case something shorts in a disasterous way, but so far I haven't found any red flags with how the board was designed. The power planes look well-enough insulated from each other, and if the Arduino can source enough current for the thermocouple sensors and their ICs, this is worth testing.
